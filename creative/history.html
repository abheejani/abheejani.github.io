History & Ideas — 5000 BCE– 2025 CE

Egypt

Indus

China

Yangshao → Longshan → Xia (legendary) → Shang → Zhou → Qin → Han → Tang → Song → Ming → Qing → PRC

Anatolia

Hattians → Hittites → Phrygia → Lydia → Byzantium

Aegean

Minoans → Mycenaeans → Classical Greece → Hellenistic → Roman Greece → Byzantium

Germanic / Celts

Celts → Germanic tribes → Goths/Franks/Vandals → Holy Roman Empire → Medieval Europe

Norse

Norse → Vikings → Denmark/Norway/Sweden → Christian Kingdoms

Steppe

Scythians → Huns → Turks → Mongols → Timurids → Mughals

Iran / Persia

Islamic World

Rashidun → Umayyad → Abbasid → Ottoman → Modern Islamic states

Rome

Republic → Empire → Byzantium (East) → Holy Roman Empire (West legacy)

Europe

Medieval Kingdoms → Renaissance → Enlightenment → Modern Europe → EU

Africa

Nubia → Axum → Ghana → Mali → Songhai → Ethiopia → Swahili states

Americas

Russia

Kievan Rus → Mongol Yoke → Tsardom → Russian Empire → USSR → Russian Federation

USA

Colonial America → 13 Colonies → United States → Civil War → Modern USA

5000–4900 BCE

Ubaid (Mesopotamia)

At Eridu, irrigation villages cluster around the temple precinct. Early communal storage facilities hint at centralized religious authority and long-distance trade in obsidian from Anatolia.

Egypt (Predynastic)

In Merimde, villages expand with wheat and barley cultivation. Black-topped pottery and simple burials show growing social complexity along the Nile floodplain.

South Asia (Mehrgarh)

Mehrgarh herders combine cattle domestication with barley farming. Bead workshops and drilled human teeth illustrate advanced craft and medical practices.

China (Yangshao)

Millet-based agriculture sustains settlements in the Yellow River basin. Painted pottery and clan cemeteries reinforce communal ritual traditions.

4900–4800 BCE

Ubaid

At Eridu, temple expansion accelerates as irrigation canals extend cultivation. Clay stamp seals circulate to mark ownership, foreshadowing the administrative systems of Sumer.

Egypt

Farmers in the Fayum Basin refine cereal cultivation. Burials with cosmetic palettes and ivory combs suggest rising social ranks within villages.

South Asia

Communities in Baluchistan herd zebu cattle and cultivate wheat. By 4800 BCE, carnelian beads appear in trade networks stretching toward Iran.

China

Yangshao kilns produce red and black painted pottery at scale. Hemp weaving spreads alongside pig domestication and millet farming.

4800–4700 BCE

Ubaid

Tripartite temple layouts emerge in southern Mesopotamia, precursors to later ziggurats. Larger settlements consolidate farming communities under religious authority.

Egypt

Merimde graves now include figurines and pottery offerings. Agricultural surpluses sustain village populations along the Nile Valley.

South Asia

Mehrgarh beadmakers craft ornaments from carnelian and turquoise. Specialized workshops supply both local and regional elites.

China

Yangshao pit houses cluster into hamlets with central courtyards. Early jade pendants appear in ritual contexts, tied to elite burials.

4700–4600 BCE

Ubaid

Clay tokens circulate for accounting, evolving into proto-records. Temple leaders consolidate control over irrigation and surplus distribution.

Egypt

Along the Nile, Predynastic graves feature palettes carved with animal motifs. Boat transport expands regional exchange.

South Asia

Baluchistan sites expand, with cattle herding and barley farming dominant. Copper tools begin supplementing stone equipment.

China

Yangshao pottery kilns multiply, producing vessels for both household and ritual use. Millet remains the agricultural backbone.

4600–4500 BCE

Ubaid

Eridu’s temple platform grows in scale, symbolizing religious-political authority. Gulf trade brings shell and stone inlays for elite goods.

Egypt (Badarian)

The Badarian culture emerges: high-fired pottery, ivory figurines, and linen textiles accompany elite burials in Upper Egypt.

South Asia

Chalcolithic copper tools spread among farming villages. Regional craft traditions link Baluchistan and the Indus plains.

China

Clan cemeteries with jade ornaments and axes mark increasing hierarchy. Agricultural intensification supports settlement growth.

4500–4400 BCE

Southern Mesopotamia

Temple-centered towns expand; bullae filled with tokens record grain rations. Obsidian and copper circulate through long-distance trade.

Nile Valley

Naqada I cemeteries yield decorated pottery, combs, and palettes. Iconography of boats and rulers begins to spread.

Levant & Anatolia

Copper smelting spreads in highland settlements. Exchange routes link obsidian, salt, and livestock between coastal and inland towns.

4400–4300 BCE

Mesopotamia

Cylinder seals emerge, used to secure storerooms and contracts. Temple precincts dominate urban planning in Sumerian towns.

Egypt

Naqada I culture expands along the Nile. Nubian gold and obsidian from Ethiopia flow north via trade.

Levant

Hilltop villages adopt fortifications; metallurgy intensifies with copper sickles and ornaments.

China

Large Yangshao ceremonial centers arise. Elite graves contain jade pendants and painted pottery.

4300–4200 BCE

Sumer

Proto-urban centers like Uruk emerge. Temple economies control grain redistribution and craft production.

Egypt

Naqada I cemeteries reveal boat iconography on pottery. Elite graves suggest rulers consolidating regional authority.

South Asia

Chalcolithic villages in Gujarat and Sindh experiment with copper tools. Bead-making workshops expand regional exchange.

Anatolia

Highland mining towns trade copper and obsidian southward. Ritual shrines remain central in settlement life.

4200–4100 BCE

Uruk (Sumer)

Mass-produced bevel-rim bowls distribute rations to laborers. Proto-cuneiform tablets begin tracking goods in temple archives.

Egypt

Naqada II pottery and palettes circulate widely via Nile trade. Local nomes consolidate into larger proto-states.

Indus

Settlements expand in Baluchistan and Sindh. Copper tools, beads, and ceramics indicate craft specialization.

China

Large Yangshao villages thrive, centered on millet farming. Jade objects enter clan rituals and elite burials.

4100–4000 BCE

Sumer

Uruk grows into the largest Mesopotamian city. Monumental temples rise, and proto-writing multiplies in administrative records.

Egypt

Naqada II iconography spreads: smiting motifs and boat scenes prefigure pharaonic kingship. Cemeteries expand with elite tombs.

Indus

Organized towns appear with standardized bricks. Trade with Mesopotamia intensifies, seen in Indus-style artifacts abroad.

Anatolia

Copper metallurgy flourishes in highland settlements. Painted pottery traditions diversify across the plateau.

4000–3900 BCE

Mesopotamia (Uruk)

Uruk expands rapidly; bevel-rim bowls standardize rations. Proto-cuneiform appears in temple archives to track goods.

Egypt (Naqada I–II)

Elites at Hierakonpolis build decorated tombs. Pottery and palettes spread through Nile trade, showing early state formation.

Indus

Villages in Sindh and Baluchistan expand. Standardized bricks and copper tools suggest growing urban planning.

Iran (Susiana)

Susa founded (~4000 BCE), with temple platforms and painted pottery linking highlands to Mesopotamia.

3900–3800 BCE

Sumer (Uruk)

Fortifications rise at Uruk. Cylinder seals secure storerooms, marking private property and contracts.

Egypt (Naqada II)

Elite burials with lapis and gold appear. Chiefs in Upper Egypt consolidate political authority along the Nile.

Iran (Susiana)

Susa temples expand; pottery motifs show Mesopotamian influence. Early administrative practices emerge.

Europe (Megaliths)

Dolmens appear in Brittany and Iberia. Massive stones require organized communal labor and ritual planning.

3800–3700 BCE

Sumer

Uruk grows into the largest city in Mesopotamia. Early city walls suggest concern with defense and control.

Egypt

Naqada culture spreads northward. Elite graves at Abydos display increasing wealth and political power.

Indus

Bead-making workshops expand. Copper tools supplement stone implements, marking a Chalcolithic transition.

China (Yangshao)

Large communal cemeteries appear. Painted pottery features elaborate designs for ritual and clan identity.

3700–3600 BCE

Sumer

Temple precincts dominate Uruk. Mass-produced bevel-rim bowls distribute rations to dependent laborers.

Egypt

Palette art shows smiting kings and animal motifs. Nile trade expands as nomes begin consolidating into proto-states.

Indus

Early towns show standardized brickwork. Craft specialization grows in ceramics and beads.

Levant

Fortified settlements rise in the Jordan Valley. Copper ore from Timna and Faynan enters trade networks.

3600–3500 BCE

Sumer (Late Uruk)

Uruk reaches 40,000+ inhabitants. Proto-writing expands, and monumental precincts like the Eanna temple rise.

Egypt (Naqada III)

Proto-hieroglyphs appear. Elite tombs at Abydos foreshadow dynastic rule.

Iran (Proto-Elamite)

Proto-Elamite culture emerges, with administrative tablets at Susa. Distinct from but parallel to Sumerian writing.

Anatolia

Metalwork expands across the plateau. Early Troy (Troy I) grows into a fortified settlement.

3500–3400 BCE

Sumer (Uruk/Jemdet Nasr)

City networks dominate Mesopotamia. Cylinder seals proliferate as tools of bureaucracy.

Egypt (Dynasty 0)

Rulers in Upper Egypt begin unification campaigns. Tombs show royal iconography of kingship.

Indus (Pre-Harappan)

Organized towns appear. Long-distance trade grows, with Indus goods reaching Mesopotamia.

Aegean

Cycladic figurines appear in island burials. Maritime exchange intensifies across the Aegean Sea.

3400–3300 BCE

Sumer

Proto-cuneiform develops into full writing. Temples manage labor, grain, and trade at unprecedented scale.

Egypt

Unification accelerates under powerful Upper Egyptian rulers. Royal symbols (crowns, smiting motifs) spread north.

Iran

Proto-Elamite script flourishes in Susa. Distinct administrative system rivals Mesopotamian models.

China (Late Yangshao)

Large villages flourish in the Yellow River basin. Painted pottery reaches its artistic peak.

3300–3200 BCE

Sumer (Uruk)

Uruk dominates southern Mesopotamia. Monumental ziggurats rise, and city networks integrate hinterlands.

Egypt

Proto-hieroglyphs on tags and labels record goods. Kingship rituals expand, with rulers as divine figures.

Indus

Early Harappan towns in Sindh and Punjab organize into proto-urban centers. Seals and craft specialization spread.

Anatolia

Troy I grows into a fortified citadel. Bronze metallurgy advances across the plateau.

3200–3100 BCE

Sumer

Uruk dominates southern Mesopotamia with trade links to Iran and Anatolia. Writing matures into true cuneiform.

Egypt (Dynasty 0)

Kings at Abydos consolidate rule. Elite tombs feature funerary goods and royal symbols.

Indus

Pre-Harappan towns like Harappa emerge. Standardized bricks and seals appear.

Aegean

Cycladic culture flourishes with marble figurines. Seafaring communities expand maritime networks.

3100–3000 BCE

Sumer

Cuneiform fully established. City-states like Uruk, Kish, and Ur dominate Mesopotamia.

Egypt (Early Dynastic)

King Narmer unifies Upper and Lower Egypt (~3100 BCE). Memphis founded as the new capital.

Indus

Harappan towns expand with urban layouts and standardized seals. Long-distance trade intensifies.

Iran (Proto-Elamite)

Proto-Elamite script flourishes. Administrative networks at Susa rival Sumerian systems.

3000–2900 BCE

Egypt (1st Dynasty)

Early dynastic pharaohs establish centralized administration at Memphis. Royal tombs at Abydos display elaborate funerary goods.

Sumer

City-states like Ur, Uruk, and Kish flourish. The earliest king lists record dynastic succession.

Indus (Early Harappan)

Urban planning intensifies at Harappa. Seals and standardized weights hint at central administration.

Aegean

Cycladic culture produces distinctive marble figurines. Early seafaring expands trade across the islands.

2900–2800 BCE

Sumer (Early Dynastic I)

Independent city-states thrive; temples serve as both religious and economic hubs. The Epic of Gilgamesh begins oral circulation in Uruk.

Egypt (2nd Dynasty)

Power struggles erupt between northern and southern dynasties. Royal tombs expand at Saqqara.

Indus

Settlements expand in Punjab and Gujarat. Agricultural surpluses support growing populations.

Iran (Proto-Elamite)

Proto-Elamite script peaks at Susa. Iran’s administrative systems rival Sumerian models.

2800–2700 BCE

Sumer

City-states like Lagash and Ur vie for supremacy. The first dynastic wars are recorded in inscriptions.

Egypt (3rd Dynasty)

Djoser commissions the Step Pyramid at Saqqara (~2700 BCE), designed by Imhotep.

Indus

Harappan towns grow more complex. Standardized brick sizes become widespread.

China (Longshan)

Longshan culture spreads in the Yellow River basin. Black pottery and rammed-earth fortifications appear.

2700–2600 BCE

Egypt (Old Kingdom)

Djoser’s Step Pyramid sets precedent for monumental stone architecture. Centralized administration strengthens.

Sumer

Epic rulers like Gilgamesh of Uruk appear in tradition (~2700 BCE). City-states compete for supremacy.

Indus

Urban centers like Mohenjo-Daro begin to form. Craft production intensifies.

Iran

Proto-Elamite script declines, giving way to regional styles. Susa remains a major center.

2600–2500 BCE

Egypt (4th Dynasty)

Khufu builds the Great Pyramid of Giza (~2550 BCE). Monumental pyramid building peaks.

Sumer

Royal tombs at Ur reveal immense wealth. City-states codify laws and expand warfare.

Indus (Mature Harappan)

Grid-planned cities like Harappa and Mohenjo-Daro thrive. Standardized weights and measures regulate trade.

China (Longshan)

Fortified towns grow larger. Elites control prestige goods like jade and black pottery.

2500–2400 BCE

Egypt

Pyramids at Giza completed. Centralized administration at its peak; pyramid texts expand funerary traditions.

Sumer

City-states of Lagash and Umma record conflicts. Enheduanna, daughter of Sargon, writes hymns.

Indus

Drainage systems and standardized brick architecture reach their peak. Indus seals spread widely in trade.

Anatolia

Early bronze metallurgy strengthens fortified settlements. Troy I grows into a trading hub.

2400–2300 BCE

Sumer

The Stele of the Vultures commemorates victory of Eannatum of Lagash over Umma. Warfare intensifies.

Egypt

5th Dynasty pharaohs emphasize solar temples. Pyramids at Abusir and Sakkara are constructed.

Indus

Trade with Mesopotamia peaks. Indus seals appear in Sumerian cities like Ur and Lagash.

China

Longshan elites fortify hilltop settlements. Early ritual bronzes emerge in Yellow River sites.

2300–2200 BCE

Akkadian Empire

Sargon of Akkad (~2334 BCE) conquers Sumer, creating the first known empire. Akkadian language spreads.

Egypt (6th Dynasty)

Pepi II reigns over 90 years. Central authority begins to weaken near the First Intermediate Period.

Indus

Mature Harappan cities maintain peak organization. Trade with Oman and Mesopotamia flourishes.

Anatolia

Hattian culture consolidates. Proto-Hittite elites begin to dominate central Anatolia.

2200–2100 BCE

Akkadian Empire

The empire collapses after 2200 BCE due to drought and Gutian invasions. City-states briefly fragment.

Egypt

First Intermediate Period begins as central control weakens. Regional governors (nomarchs) dominate politics.

Indus

Mature Harappan cities persist. Urban planning, drainage, and trade remain sophisticated.

China

Longshan culture reaches peak influence. Early state structures form in the Yellow River valley.

2100–2000 BCE

Ur III Dynasty

Ur-Nammu establishes the Third Dynasty of Ur (~2112 BCE). The Code of Ur-Nammu becomes the earliest surviving law code.

Egypt (Middle Kingdom begins)

Theban rulers reunify Egypt under Mentuhotep II. The Middle Kingdom marks a cultural renaissance.

Indus

Signs of decline appear in western settlements. Trade with Mesopotamia decreases.

Anatolia

Hattian polities expand influence. Precursors to the Hittite state consolidate power in central Anatolia.

2000–1900 BCE

Mesopotamia (Isin-Larsa)

After the fall of Ur III, rival dynasties at Isin and Larsa compete for control. Amorite tribes settle across Mesopotamia.

Egypt (Middle Kingdom)

Senusret III expands Egyptian power into Nubia. Military campaigns fortify Egypt’s southern frontier.

Anatolia (Old Hittite)

Hattian and early Hittite states consolidate in central Anatolia. Written records appear at Kültepe.

Indus (Late Harappan)

Major cities decline. Signs of deurbanization and rural dispersal begin around 1900 BCE.

1900–1800 BCE

Babylon (Rise of Amorites)

Amorite dynasty rises in Babylon. Hammurabi’s predecessors consolidate local power.

China (Erlitou/Xia)

The Erlitou culture emerges in the Yellow River valley, linked to the legendary Xia dynasty.

Egypt

12th Dynasty continues prosperity. Fortresses line Nubia, securing trade and resources.

Anatolia

Old Hittite rulers expand through warfare. Hattusa grows as a fortified capital.

1800–1700 BCE

Babylon

Hammurabi (r. ~1792–1750 BCE) conquers Mesopotamia. His famous Code sets a precedent for law codes.

Egypt

13th Dynasty weakens. The Hyksos, Asiatic rulers, infiltrate the Delta.

China (Erlitou)

Palatial complexes and bronze workshops flourish. The earliest large-scale bronze casting begins.

Anatolia (Hittites)

The Hittite Old Kingdom consolidates. Written treaties and diplomacy mark early statecraft.

1700–1600 BCE

Babylon

Hammurabi dies (~1750 BCE). Babylon dominates Mesopotamia but fragments after his successors.

Egypt (Hyksos)

The Hyksos seize control of northern Egypt (~1650 BCE). Horse-drawn chariots appear in the Nile Valley.

China (Shang)

The Shang dynasty emerges. Early oracle bone inscriptions appear.

Aegean (Minoans)

Palatial centers rise at Knossos on Crete. Frescoes and advanced seafaring define Minoan culture.

1600–1500 BCE

Egypt (New Kingdom)

Ahmose I expels the Hyksos (~1550 BCE), founding the New Kingdom. Egypt enters its imperial zenith.

China (Shang)

Shang kings establish their capital at Anyang. Ritual bronzes and divination dominate elite culture.

Hittite Empire

The Hittite Kingdom expands, rivaling Babylon and Mitanni. Chariot warfare advances.

Aegean (Mycenaeans)

Mycenaean Greece emerges on the mainland. Fortified citadels and shaft graves mark warrior elites.

1500–1400 BCE

Egypt (18th Dynasty)

Thutmose III campaigns in the Levant, expanding Egyptian empire to its greatest reach.

China (Shang)

Oracle bone inscriptions detail divination. Bronze metallurgy reaches peak sophistication.

Hittite Empire

Expansion consolidates under strong kings. Anatolia becomes a hub of diplomacy and warfare.

Levant (Canaanites)

Canaanite city-states like Byblos and Ugarit thrive as hubs of Mediterranean trade.

1400–1300 BCE

Egypt

Akhenaten launches Atenist religious reforms (~1350 BCE). Nefertiti plays a prominent role.

China (Shang)

Royal tombs at Anyang contain human sacrifices and vast bronze hoards. Ancestor worship dominates.

Aegean

Minoan palaces decline after volcanic eruptions and invasions. Mycenaeans rise in dominance.

Levant

Ugarit reaches cultural peak, developing alphabetic writing precursors. Coastal trade flourishes.

1300–1200 BCE

Egypt (19th Dynasty)

Ramesses II (“the Great”) reigns (~1279–1213 BCE). The Battle of Kadesh against the Hittites (~1274 BCE) ends in the first recorded peace treaty.

Hittite Empire

The Hittite Empire reaches its zenith, controlling Anatolia and northern Syria. Diplomacy with Egypt shapes Near Eastern politics.

Aegean (Mycenaeans)

Mycenaean palaces dominate Greece. Epic traditions later remembered as the backdrop of the Trojan War.

Levant

Canaanite cities serve as vassals in Egyptian–Hittite rivalries. Trade and diplomacy shape the coast.

1200–1100 BCE

Aegean Collapse

The Trojan War (~1200 BCE) echoes in legend. Mycenaean palaces collapse amid invasions and internal turmoil.

Hittite Collapse

The Hittite Empire falls (~1190 BCE) as Sea Peoples and internal strife devastate Anatolia.

Egypt (20th Dynasty)

Ramesses III repels the Sea Peoples (~1177 BCE) but Egypt’s New Kingdom soon declines.

Levant (Sea Peoples)

Philistines settle on the southern coast. Canaanite cities suffer destruction and reorganization.

1100–1000 BCE

Greek Dark Age

Mycenaean palaces lie in ruins. Populations shrink; oral traditions preserve epic memory of earlier times.

Levant (Israelites)

Early Israelite highland villages emerge (~1100 BCE). Tribal confederations consolidate into a kingdom by the next century.

China (Zhou)

The Shang dynasty ends (~1046 BCE) when the Zhou overthrow them. The Mandate of Heaven is proclaimed.

Egypt

The New Kingdom collapses into the Third Intermediate Period. Regional rulers dominate politics.

1000–900 BCE

Israel & Judah

King David (~1000 BCE) establishes Jerusalem as the capital. Solomon builds the First Temple.

Neo-Assyria

Assyria resurges under Ashur-dan II, expanding into the Zagros. Iron weapons give them military dominance.

Zhou Dynasty

The Western Zhou consolidate their feudal order. The Mandate of Heaven legitimizes rule.

Greece

Early city-states begin forming. Oral traditions preserve the epics later attributed to Homer.

Phrygia

Phrygian kingdom rises in central Anatolia after the Hittite collapse. Legendary King Midas associated with this era.

900–800 BCE

Assyria

Ashurnasirpal II (~883 BCE) expands Neo-Assyrian power, moving the capital to Kalhu (Nimrud).

Phoenicians

Tyre and Sidon dominate Mediterranean trade. The Phoenician alphabet spreads via colonization.

Egypt

The Third Intermediate Period continues. Libyan dynasties rule parts of the Delta.

Greece

Polis institutions emerge. Homeric epics, the Iliad and Odyssey, are codified around this time.

Zhou

Western Zhou rulers maintain authority. Feudal lords expand agriculture across the Yellow River valley.

800–700 BCE

Assyria

Tiglath-Pileser III (~745 BCE) reforms the Assyrian army, creating the first professional standing army.

Israel & Judah

The kingdoms of Israel and Judah flourish. Prophets like Amos and Isaiah emerge later in this century.

Greece

The Olympic Games are founded (~776 BCE). City-states like Athens and Sparta consolidate.

Medes

Iranian-speaking Medes settle on the plateau, beginning to form tribal confederations.

Rome (Foundation)

Legendary founding of Rome by Romulus (~753 BCE). Early kingship period begins.

700–600 BCE

Assyria

Sennacherib and Esarhaddon expand Assyrian dominance. Nineveh becomes a magnificent capital.

Medes & Persia

Medes establish their kingdom in Iran. Early Achaemenid Persians settle in Fars.

Israel & Judah

Assyria conquers the northern Kingdom of Israel (~722 BCE); Judah survives as a vassal state.

Greece

Sparta institutes its military system under Lycurgus. Athens begins aristocratic reforms.

Rome

Etruscan kings dominate early Rome. Religious and civic traditions take root.

600–500 BCE

Persia

Cyrus the Great (~559 BCE) unites Persians and overthrows the Medes (~550 BCE), founding the Achaemenid Empire.

India

Siddhartha Gautama (the Buddha) is born (~563 BCE). The Mahajanapadas flourish in the Ganges plain.

China

Confucius (~551–479 BCE) teaches ethics and order. Sun Tzu and Laozi also flourish in this century.

Greece

Persian Wars begin (~499 BCE). Athens and Sparta lead resistance against the empire.

Rome

The Roman monarchy ends (~509 BCE), replaced by the Republic led by elected consuls.

500–400 BCE

Greece

Marathon (~490 BCE), Thermopylae (~480 BCE), and Salamis secure Greek independence. The Classical Age begins.

Persia

Darius I and Xerxes I expand the empire from India to Greece. Persepolis built as ceremonial capital.

India

The rise of Magadha sets the stage for the Mauryan Empire. Buddhism and Jainism gain followers.

China (Warring States)

The Zhou weaken, and states like Qin and Chu gain dominance. Philosophical schools flourish.

Rome

Rome expands in Italy, defeating Etruscans and Samnites. Republican institutions consolidate.

400–300 BCE

Greece

Peloponnesian War (~431–404 BCE) devastates Athens and Sparta. Macedonia rises under Philip II.

Persia

Persia remains vast but weakened. Defeated by Alexander the Great (~330 BCE).

Alexander & Hellenistic

Alexander the Great (r. ~336–323 BCE) conquers Persia and spreads Hellenistic culture across three continents.

China (Qin)

The state of Qin rises under King Zheng, preparing for unification of China.

Rome

Rome dominates central Italy. Early Punic Wars against Carthage loom ahead.

300–200 BCE

India (Maurya)

Chandragupta Maurya (~321 BCE) founds the Mauryan Empire. Ashoka (~268 BCE) spreads Buddhism.

China (Qin/Han)

Qin Shi Huang unifies China (~221 BCE). The Han Dynasty follows (~206 BCE).

Greece (Hellenistic)

Alexander’s empire fragments into Seleucid, Ptolemaic, and Antigonid kingdoms.

Rome

Rome fights the Punic Wars (~264–201 BCE) against Carthage. Hannibal crosses the Alps.

Persia

The Seleucid Empire controls much of Persia after Alexander, but local dynasties challenge them.

200–100 BCE

Han China

Han dynasty expands into Central Asia, establishing the Silk Road. Emperor Wu strengthens Confucian state ideology.

Rome

Rome defeats Carthage in the Second Punic War (~202 BCE). Rome begins dominating the Mediterranean.

India

The Maurya Empire declines after Ashoka. Regional kingdoms rise.

Greece

Hellenistic kingdoms decline. Rome asserts dominance in Greece after the Battle of Pydna (~168 BCE).

Persia

Seleucid control collapses. Parthians rise, seizing Persia by ~141 BCE.

100–1 BCE

Rome

Julius Caesar conquers Gaul (~58–50 BCE), then is assassinated in 44 BCE. Augustus establishes the Empire in 27 BCE.

Han China

Han expands into Korea and Vietnam. Silk Road trade flourishes with Rome and Central Asia.

India

Indo-Greek and Mauryan successor states flourish in the northwest. Bactrian influence grows.

Parthian Persia

Parthians dominate Persia and Mesopotamia, clashing with Rome for centuries to come.

Greece

Greece absorbed into the Roman sphere after Corinth destroyed (~146 BCE). Greek culture endures under Roman rule.

1–100 CE

Rome

Augustus inaugurates the Pax Romana (~27 BCE–14 CE). Tiberius and Nero follow; the Colosseum begins construction.

Levant (Christianity)

Jesus of Nazareth (~4 BCE–30 CE) preaches in Judea. Christianity spreads through apostles like Paul.

Han China

Han Dynasty reaches peak under Emperor Guangwu. The Silk Road connects Rome and China.

India (Kushan rise)

Kushan tribes migrate into north India, setting stage for a future empire. Buddhism spreads along trade routes.

Parthia

Parthians battle Rome for Armenia and Mesopotamia. Trade via Silk Road flourishes.

100–200 CE

Rome

Trajan (~98–117 CE) expands empire to its maximum size. Hadrian builds frontier walls in Britain.

Han China

Later Han faces corruption and peasant unrest. Inventions like paper revolutionize bureaucracy.

Kushan Empire

Kanishka (~127 CE) rules a vast Kushan realm. Buddhism thrives in Gandhara art.

Parthia

Rome clashes with Parthians over Mesopotamia. Trade goods like silk and spices funnel through Persia.

Christianity

Persecutions intensify under Marcus Aurelius. Early churches spread across the Mediterranean.

200–300 CE

Rome (Crisis of the Third Century)

Military anarchy and 26 emperors in 50 years. Plagues and invasions weaken the empire.

China (Three Kingdoms)

Han collapses (~220 CE), replaced by Wei, Shu, and Wu. Era immortalized in Romance of the Three Kingdoms.

India

Kushan Empire declines. Regional kingdoms and Gupta precursors emerge.

Sassanian Persia

Ardashir I (~224 CE) overthrows Parthians, founding the Sassanian Empire. Zoroastrianism formalized.

Christianity

Church Fathers like Tertullian and Origen write. Persecutions continue across Rome.

300–400 CE

Rome

Constantine (~312 CE) legalizes Christianity. Founding of Constantinople (~330 CE) reshapes empire.

Sassanian Persia

Shapur II defeats Romans and expands east. Zoroastrian clergy dominate court life.

China (Jin Dynasty)

Western Jin unites China (~280 CE) but collapses into chaos by 316 CE. Northern nomads invade.

India (Gupta rise)

Early Gupta dynasty consolidates Magadha. Hinduism and Sanskrit culture revive strongly.

Christianity

Council of Nicaea (~325 CE) defines orthodoxy. Christianity spreads as Rome’s state religion.

400–500 CE

Rome (Fall of West)

476 CE: Romulus Augustulus deposed by Odoacer. Western Roman Empire collapses.

China (Northern & Southern Dynasties)

China splits into rival dynasties. Buddhism spreads widely via Silk Road monks.

India (Gupta Empire)

Chandragupta II leads the Gupta golden age. Advances in math, astronomy, and literature flourish.

Sassanian Persia

Persia battles Eastern Rome. Zoroastrianism influences law and statecraft.

Europe (Barbarian Kingdoms)

Visigoths, Vandals, and Franks establish kingdoms in former Roman lands.

500–600 CE

Byzantine Empire

Justinian I (~527–565 CE) reconquers Italy, codifies Roman law, and builds Hagia Sophia.

India

Gupta Empire collapses after Hun invasions. Regional states dominate the subcontinent.

China (Sui unification)

After centuries of division, the Sui Dynasty reunifies China (~589 CE).

Sassanian Persia

Khosrow I reforms taxation and military. Persia rivals Byzantium in strength.

Europe

Merovingian Franks consolidate Gaul under Clovis. Christianity spreads in the west.

600–700 CE

Islam

Muhammad (~570–632 CE) founds Islam. The Rashidun Caliphate expands across Arabia and beyond.

Byzantine Empire

Byzantines defend against Persian invasions. Later face the first Arab conquests.

Sassanian Persia

Defeated by Arabs (~651 CE), ending the dynasty. Persia becomes part of the Islamic Caliphate.

China (Tang rise)

The Tang Dynasty founded (~618 CE). Chang’an grows into the world’s largest city.

Europe

Lombards settle in Italy. Anglo-Saxon kingdoms consolidate in Britain.

700–800 CE

Islamic Caliphate

The Umayyads rule from Damascus, stretching empire from Spain to India. Overthrown by Abbasids (~750 CE).

Tang China

Tang golden age: poetry of Li Bai and Du Fu. Silk Road thrives with trade.

Franks

Charlemagne consolidates Carolingian power in Gaul and Germany. Crowned emperor later in 800.

Byzantine Empire

Iconoclast controversies erupt. Byzantium struggles against Arab and Bulgar threats.

India

Regional kingdoms like the Chalukyas and Pallavas dominate. Hindu temple architecture expands.

800–900 CE

Carolingian Empire

Charlemagne crowned “Emperor of the Romans” (~800 CE). Carolingian Renaissance begins.

Abbasid Caliphate

Baghdad becomes a hub of science and culture. Scholars preserve Greek philosophy and advance medicine.

Tang China

Height of Tang culture. Porcelain, poetry, and Buddhist influence reach new heights.

Vikings

Norse seafarers raid Britain, France, and beyond. Settlements appear in Ireland and Iceland.

Byzantine Empire

Byzantines recover under Basil I. Missionaries spread Christianity to the Slavs.

900–1000 CE

Vikings

Viking Age peaks. Norse explorers reach Greenland; Leif Erikson sails to Vinland (~1000 CE).

Islamic Caliphates

Abbasids weaken; Fatimids rise in Egypt. Baghdad remains a scientific center.

China (Song rise)

Tang fall (~907 CE); Song Dynasty begins (~960 CE). Printing and gunpowder technology develop.

Europe

Holy Roman Empire forms under Otto I (~962 CE). Feudalism spreads in Europe.

Byzantine Empire

Macedonian dynasty revives strength. Orthodox Christianity consolidates in the east.

1000–1100 CE

Europe

Feudal kingdoms dominate. William the Conqueror invades England in 1066.

Crusades

The First Crusade (~1096–1099) captures Jerusalem. Christian states established in the Levant.

Islam (Seljuks)

Seljuk Turks dominate the Middle East, defeating Byzantines at Manzikert (1071).

Song China

Song dynasty thrives with gunpowder, printing, and urban commerce. Population exceeds 100 million.

Byzantine Empire

Byzantines weaken after Manzikert; Anatolian heartlands lost to Turks.

1100–1200 CE

Europe

High Middle Ages. Gothic cathedrals rise; universities founded in Paris, Bologna, Oxford.

Crusades

Second and Third Crusades unfold. Richard the Lionheart and Saladin clash.

Islam (Ayyubids)

Saladin retakes Jerusalem in 1187. Ayyubid dynasty dominates Egypt and Syria.

Song China

Song face constant threats from Jurchen Jin dynasty. Move capital south to Hangzhou.

India

Delhi Sultanate begins (~1192) after Turkic conquests at Tarain.

1200–1300 CE

Mongol Empire

Genghis Khan (~1206) unites steppe tribes. Mongols conquer from China to Persia.

Yuan China

Kublai Khan founds Yuan dynasty (~1271). Marco Polo visits court at Khanbaliq.

India (Delhi Sultanate)

Sultanate consolidates northern India under Muslim rule. Architecture like Qutb Minar rises.

Islamic World

Baghdad destroyed by Mongols (~1258), ending Abbasid central authority.

Europe

Crusades wane. Scholasticism thrives with Aquinas. Mongol trade links open to the West.

1300–1400 CE

Europe

The Black Death (~1347–1351) kills up to 1/3 of Europe. Hundred Years’ War begins (~1337).

Persia (Ilkhanate)

Mongol Ilkhanate rules Persia. Later collapses into local dynasties.

China (Yuan/Ming)

Yuan dynasty declines; Zhu Yuanzhang founds Ming dynasty (~1368).

India

Delhi Sultanate endures invasions by Timur (~1398). Regional kingdoms rise.

Islam

Mamluks control Egypt. Trade with Europe intensifies through Mediterranean.

1400–1500 CE

Europe (Renaissance)

Florence and Italy flourish with Leonardo and Michelangelo. Gutenberg’s press (~1450).

Ottomans

Mehmed II conquers Constantinople in 1453, ending Byzantium.

Ming China

Zheng He voyages across the Indian Ocean (~1405–1433).

India

Delhi Sultanate fragments. Regional sultanates dominate until Mughal rise.

Inca Empire

Incas consolidate Andes, expanding across Peru and Ecuador.

1500–1600 CE

Europe (Reformation)

Luther posts 95 Theses (~1517). Protestant Reformation divides Christendom.

Ottoman Empire

Ottomans expand under Suleiman the Magnificent (~1520–1566).

Mughal India

Babur founds Mughal dynasty (~1526). Akbar expands empire and integrates Hindus.

Ming China

Ming Great Wall reinforced. Jesuit missions bring European science.

Americas

Cortés conquers Aztec Empire (~1521); Pizarro defeats Inca (~1532).

1600–1700 CE

Europe

Scientific Revolution: Galileo, Kepler, and Newton transform knowledge. Thirty Years’ War (~1618–1648) devastates Central Europe.

Ottoman Empire

Ottoman expansion slows. Failed Siege of Vienna (1683) marks turning point.

Mughal India

Shah Jahan commissions Taj Mahal (1632). Aurangzeb expands empire to its peak.

China (Qing rise)

Manchus capture Beijing (1644) and found the Qing dynasty, ruling until 1911.

Persia (Safavids)

Safavid dynasty under Abbas I fosters culture and trade in Isfahan.

1700–1800 CE

Europe (Enlightenment)

Voltaire, Rousseau, and Kant shape new ideas. American Revolution (1776) and French Revolution (1789).

India

Mughal Empire declines. British East India Company asserts growing control.

China (Qing)

Qianlong Emperor oversees prosperity. Population surges past 300 million.

Ottoman Empire

Ottomans continue gradual decline. Russia and Austria expand at their expense.

Persia

Safavid dynasty collapses (1736). Afsharid and Zand dynasties rise in succession.

1800–1900 CE

Europe

Napoleonic Wars reshape Europe (1803–1815). Industrial Revolution spreads. 1848 revolutions challenge monarchies.

India

Sepoy Rebellion (1857) ends Mughal shadow rule. British Raj established.

China (Qing)

Opium Wars (1839–1860) force concessions. Taiping Rebellion (1850–1864) devastates population.

Ottoman Empire

Tanzimat reforms attempt modernization. The empire weakens, called the “Sick Man of Europe.”

Persia (Qajar)

Qajar dynasty rules. European concessions increase foreign influence.

Americas

U.S. expands westward; Civil War (1861–1865) abolishes slavery. Latin America undergoes independence struggles.

1900–2000 CE

Europe / World Wars

World War I (1914–1918) devastates Europe. World War II (1939–1945) reshapes global order.

Americas

U.S. becomes global superpower. Cold War with USSR dominates world politics (1947–1991).

India

Independence in 1947. Partition creates India and Pakistan. Gandhi and Nehru shape new nation.

China

Qing collapse (1911). Communist victory under Mao (1949). PRC established.

Middle East

Ottoman Empire collapses (1918). Arab states emerge; Israel founded (1948).

Iran

Pahlavi dynasty modernizes Persia. Islamic Revolution in 1979 creates Islamic Republic.

2000–2025 CE

Europe

EU expands eastward (~2004). Eurozone crisis (~2009) challenges unity. Brexit (2016) reshapes integration.

India

Rapid growth follows liberalization. Nuclear tests (~1998–2001) show deterrence. Modi elected (~2014) strengthens Hindu nationalism.

China

WTO entry (2001) accelerates growth. Beijing Olympics (2008) mark global arrival. Belt & Road (~2013) extends influence.

Middle East

9/11 (2001) sparks U.S. wars in Afghanistan and Iraq. Arab Spring (~2011) topples regimes from Tunisia to Egypt, sparks Syrian war.

Iran

Green Movement protests (2009). Nuclear program leads to JCPOA deal (2015). Tensions spike again after 2018.

Americas

9/11 reshapes U.S. policy. Financial crisis (2008), Trump presidency (2016), and COVID-19 pandemic (2020) define era.

Russia

Putin dominates politics since 2000. Annexation of Crimea (2014), invasion of Ukraine (2022) strain global order.
